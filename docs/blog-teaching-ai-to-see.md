# Teaching an AI to See Like an Illustrator

*How three sessions of spectacular failure led to a pipeline that actually works*

---

There's a moment in every ambitious project where you realize you've been solving the wrong problem. For us, that moment came on attempt number seven of getting Claude to trace a mechanical tower — when the evaluator harness scored the output 4.6 out of 10, and the human scored it 0.3.

Not a rounding error. A 15x calibration gap. The AI thought it was doing B-minus work. The illustrator thought it was doing F-minus work. Both were looking at the same image — a tangle of lines placed with the confidence of someone giving directions in a city they've never visited.

This is the story of how we figured out why, and what we built instead.

---

## The Problem That Wouldn't Stay Solved

The setup sounds simple enough: you have a reference image of a mechanical tower (generated by Midjourney, so already playing fast and loose with structural anatomy). You want an AI to trace it — not pixel-perfect reproduction, but the kind of intelligent tracing an illustrator does. Identify the front face, the side face, the top. Draw clean vector paths that follow construction geometry, not shadow edges.

We tried. Seven times across three sessions. The scores:

- Hand trace: 0.2/10
- Head trace: 0.3/10
- Tower trace: 0.002/10

Zero point zero zero two. Out of ten.

Each attempt taught us something, though mostly what we learned was variations on "this doesn't work." Direct coordinate placement? LLMs can't do it — the embedding distance between 6.999 and 7.000 is nearly double that of 6.500 and 6.501. It's tokenization all the way down. Building a 54-file knowledge base about anatomy, geometry, and Loomis/Vilppu/Bridgman constructivist drawing methods? The knowledge was correct. The output was still wrong. Knowledge isn't ability.

The most instructive failure was the constraint-based pipeline. We built a contour scanner with multi-exposure edge voting (form edges persist across brightness levels, shadows don't — clever, right?). A contour labeler that classifies edges as silhouette, face boundary, panel line, or shadow. A tonal analyzer doing k-means zone segmentation. A constraint solver that maps semantic labels to pixel coordinates.

133 tests. All passing.

The pipeline found 14,000 form edge pixels on the tower. Then assembled them into a trace that scored zero.

The problem wasn't finding edges. The problem was that edge detection — any edge detection, learned or classical — sees brightness transitions. It cannot distinguish between a form edge (where the actual 3D geometry turns away from view) and a shadow edge (where light creates an apparent boundary). Both look identical in 2D. Both are brightness transitions. One is structural. One is lighting.

To know the difference, you need to understand the 3D shape. And you can't understand the 3D shape from 2D edges, because the shadow edges are corrupting your signal.

This is a chicken-and-egg problem, and you can't iterate your way out of it.

---

## The Research That Changed the Direction

At this point we did what we should have done first: read the literature. Thirty papers on spatial reasoning in LLMs. A survey of every image-to-3D system shipping in 2026. The landscape had shifted dramatically.

The headline findings:

**Single-image-to-3D is production-ready.** TRELLIS.2 (Microsoft, 4B parameters, open source) reconstructs a mesh from a single image in three seconds. Sharp mechanical features. Handles exactly the kind of geometric subjects that make organic-focused models stumble.

**LLMs architecturally cannot output precise coordinates.** This isn't a training data problem. The tokenizer encodes numbers in ways that create irrational distance metrics between neighboring values. Every successful spatial AI system — every single one — uses LLMs for semantics and external solvers for geometry.

**The path through 3D has no shadows.** A mesh has geometry. Period. No lighting artifacts, no ambient occlusion, no cast shadows pretending to be edges. Once you have the mesh, form edges are known intrinsically — they're the silhouette boundaries of the actual geometry.

The answer was obvious once you saw it: stop trying to distinguish form from shadow in 2D. Reconstruct the 3D shape. Project it back to 2D. The shadows disappear.

---

## What We Built

The pipeline chains seven new modules, each doing one thing well:

**Reference Image** enters the pipeline and goes to TRELLIS.2, which reconstructs a 3D mesh. The mesh goes to the face grouper, which clusters mesh faces by their normal direction — faces pointing the same way belong together. The front of the tower is one group. The side is another. The top is another. This is something an illustrator does intuitively (identify the major planes) but that no amount of 2D edge detection can reliably automate.

Each face group's boundary gets projected back to 2D, matching the camera angle of the original reference. These projected contours are clean — they follow actual geometry, not lighting.

Then comes the part that makes this more than a one-shot pipeline: the **feedback loop**.

The projected contours get scored against the reference image. Where they don't match, the displacement is measured. A damped correction shifts each point 30% of the way toward where it should be (full correction overshoots; we learned this the hard way). A gradient-based optimizer refines further. The final displacement vectors get stored.

Next time the pipeline runs — on the same image or a different one — those stored corrections are pre-applied to the initial projection. Each run makes the next one better.

This is the same pattern that worked for DWPose skeleton correction: detect the skeleton, the user corrects it, the system learns the correction pattern, future skeletons get pre-corrected. Except now it operates on projected face boundaries instead of joint positions.

---

## The Evaluator Problem

We also fixed the evaluator. The original scoring used a subjective multi-factor blend — part count, color separation, symmetry. It produced a 4.6/10 on work that was actually 0.3/10.

The replacement uses Hausdorff distance: for each point on the AI-generated trace, find the nearest point on the reference trace, measure the gap. Average those gaps. Normalize by the image diagonal. Score = max(0, 1 - mean_deviation / reference_scale).

Identical traces score 1.0. Completely disjoint traces score ~0. A trace shifted by 10% of the image scores 0.9. The math doesn't lie, and it doesn't have an opinion.

This turned out to be important for the feedback loop — you can't correct what you can't measure, and you can't measure with a ruler that reads "pretty good" when the answer is "completely wrong."

---

## What Didn't Change

The tools from the earlier sessions weren't wasted. The multi-exposure edge voting, the contour labeler, the tonal analyzer — they're still there, still tested, still passing. They solve real problems (form-vs-shadow distinction in 2D is genuinely helped by multi-exposure voting). They just can't solve the whole problem alone.

The 54-file creation skill graph is still there too. It's a debugger, not a teacher — useful for catching Midjourney's anatomy errors (MJ gives mechs extra shoulders sometimes) but it doesn't make the AI better at spatial reasoning. Knowledge doesn't transfer to ability. But it does transfer to diagnosis.

The DWPose integration still works. The correction learning system we built for joint-level deltas now also handles projection deltas. Same pattern, different data.

## The Numbers

- **7 new modules**, 7,318 lines of code
- **137 tests**, all passing (2 skipped — they require DiffVG's compiled C++ module)
- **8 modified modules** (evaluator, correction learning, auto-correct, orchestrator, contour-to-path, DiffVG, confidence scorer, dependency config)
- **0 external API calls** — everything runs locally, no cloud services
- **3 seconds** for mesh reconstruction on GPU (60 seconds on CPU)

The pipeline hasn't been tested end-to-end on the tower yet — that requires downloading TRELLIS.2's model weights. But the feedback loop works on synthetic geometry (cubes, projected faces), and each component is independently verified.

The interesting test will be whether a Midjourney-rendered mech — with its non-photorealistic lighting, its impossible anatomy, its stylized proportions — produces a usable mesh. The research says TRELLIS.2 handles "sharp mechanical features" well. We'll see.

## What This Means

The deeper lesson isn't about 3D reconstruction or differentiable rendering. It's about the boundary between what LLMs can and can't do.

LLMs are excellent at semantics. "This is the front face. That shadow is cast by the overhang. The top plate is foreshortened because we're looking from below." All correct. All useful. All worthless without spatial precision.

Spatial precision requires external tools — meshes, solvers, renderers, distance metrics. The LLM's job is to understand *what* to do. The solver's job is to compute *where* to put it.

Every successful spatial AI system in 2026 follows this split. The ones that try to have the LLM do both produce work that scores 0.002 out of 10.

We're not the first to learn this. But we might be the first to build a self-correcting loop on top of it — where the gap between "what the LLM understood" and "where the solver placed it" becomes training data for the next run.

That's the part that feels genuinely new. Not the reconstruction. Not the projection. The learning.
