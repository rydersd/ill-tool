## 1. Andrew Loomis

**Key texts**: *Drawing the Head and Hands* (1956), *Figure Drawing for All It's Worth* (1943)

### 1.1 Head Construction: The Ball-and-Jaw Method

**Core principle**: The head is a sphere (cranium) with a jaw block attached. The sphere provides 3D rotation; the jaw provides the only movable mass.

#### Step-by-step construction order:

1. **Draw a sphere** representing the cranium (brain case only).
2. **Place the cross**: A vertical line (center/nose line) and horizontal line (brow line) divide the sphere into 4 quadrants. These lines curve around the sphere surface as meridian and equator.
3. **Flatten the sides**: Slice off sections on each side of the sphere where the ears attach. The side plane width is approximately 2/3 of the sphere radius.
4. **Mark the cross intersection**: Where the vertical and horizontal lines meet on the front face. This is the primary anchor point.
5. **Divide vertically into thirds** (from brow line downward):
   - Brow line to hairline = 1 unit (hairline sits midway between brow and top of skull)
   - Brow line to nose base = 1 unit
   - Nose base to chin = 1 unit
6. **Attach the jaw block**: Draw lines from the ear area converging toward the chin point. The jaw is the only independently movable bone.
7. **Place features using the thirds**:
   - Eyes: On the horizontal center of the entire head (brow line is above eye line)
   - Nose: Base at the bottom of the second third
   - Mouth: Approximately 1/3 up from chin to nose base
   - Ears: Bottom-back quadrant of the flattened side plane; horizontally aligned with nose base

#### Programmable ratios:

| Measurement | Value |
|---|---|
| Head vertical division | 4 equal units: top-to-hairline, hairline-to-brow, brow-to-nose, nose-to-chin |
| Eye line position | Vertical center of total head height |
| Eye spacing | Distance between eyes = width of one eye (front view) |
| Inner eye corners | Align vertically with nostril edges |
| Mouth placement | ~2/3 of the way up from chin to nose base |
| Side plane width | ~2/3 of sphere radius |
| Ear vertical span | From brow line to nose base |

#### 3D rotation rules (Prokopenko extension of Loomis):

- **Y-axis rotation (left/right turn)**: As head turns away, the visible front-plane oval narrows; the side-plane oval widens. The center vertical line shifts toward the visible edge.
- **X-axis rotation (up/down tilt)**: The horizontal thirds foreshorten (compress) on extreme up/down tilts. Looking down compresses upper thirds; looking up compresses lower thirds.
- **Z-axis rotation (head roll)**: The angle of the center line and the angle/placement of the side-plane oval on the sphere establish roll.
- **Foreshortening rule**: Nose base and ear base align horizontally at eye level. Head tilted back: nose base moves above ear line. Head tilted forward: nose base moves below ear line. Greater angle = greater separation.

### 1.2 Figure Construction: The 8-Head Mannequin

**Core principle**: The idealized figure is 8 heads tall. Anatomy comes AFTER proportion; proportion comes AFTER mass and action.

#### Vertical landmarks (in head-height units):

| Unit | Male Landmark |
|---|---|
| 0-1 | Top of head to chin |
| 1-2 | Chin to nipple line |
| 2-3 | Nipple line to navel |
| 3-4 | Navel to crotch (HALFWAY POINT of total figure) |
| 4-5 | Crotch to mid-thigh |
| 5-6 | Mid-thigh to below knee |
| 6-7 | Below knee to mid-lower-leg |
| 7-8 | Mid-lower-leg to sole of foot |

#### Width proportions:

| Measurement | Male | Female |
|---|---|---|
| Shoulder width | 2 1/3 heads | 2 heads |
| Hip width | 1.5 heads | 1.5 heads |
| Waist width | ~1 1/3 heads | 1 head |
| Upper torso width | 1 3/4 heads | 1 1/2 heads |

#### Geometric shape assignments:

| Body Part | Geometric Primitive | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Head | Sphere + jaw block | See head construction above |
| Neck | Cylinder | Tapers slightly toward head |
| Ribcage | Tilted egg / barrel | ~2 heads tall, 1 3/4 heads wide, 1 1/3 heads deep |
| Pelvis | Shallow bowl / smaller oval | ~1 head tall, 1 1/4 heads wide, 1 head deep |
| Upper arm | Tapered cylinder | 1 1/2 heads long, 1/4 head wide at thickest |
| Forearm | Tapered cylinder | ~1 head long (elbow to wrist), 1/3 head wide |
| Hand | ~3/4 head long | |
| Upper leg (thigh) | Tapered cylinder | ~2 heads long |
| Lower leg + foot | Tapered cylinder + block | ~2 heads combined |
| Foot length | ~1 head length | |

#### Construction order:

1. Establish gesture line (the action/movement)
2. Place head as oval with cross guidelines
3. Divide total height into 8 equal head-height segments
4. Place ribcage oval below head (centered on spine line)
5. Place pelvis oval below ribcage
6. Connect with spine curve and neck cylinder
7. Construct arm cylinders (decreasing diameter toward wrists)
8. Construct leg cylinders in three sections (thigh, calf, foot)
9. Add joint indicators (shoulders as spheres, elbows/knees as hinges)

#### Arm reach landmarks:
- Wrists fall slightly below the crotch
- Fingertips reach to mid-thigh
- Elbows align with the navel/waist

## 2. Glenn Vilppu

**Key texts**: *Vilppu Drawing Manual* (2012/2021), Vilppu Academy courses, New Masters Academy lecture series

### 2.1 Core Philosophy

**"There are no rules, just tools."** Unlike Loomis's measurement-first approach, Vilppu treats geometric primitives as analytical tools applied flexibly, not as rigid templates.

**Critical distinction**: Vilppu's method is a PIPELINE (gesture first, structure second), not a template (structure first, gesture fitted in).

### 2.2 The Gesture-to-Construction Pipeline

#### Phase 1: Gesture (the skeleton of movement)

**What to capture**: The movement that connects contours, forms, and tones -- NOT the outline.

**Line vocabulary (CSI system)**:
- **C curve**: Single-direction arc. Used for relaxed, flowing forms.
- **S curve**: Reversal curve ("line of beauty"). Maximum expression. Used for dynamic, twisting forms.
- **Straight line (I)**: For tension, rigidity, structural emphasis.

**Rules**:
1. Find the LONGEST AXIS first (down the length of torso, leg, or arm)
2. Establish ONE main line of action connecting head through body to ground contact
3. Use ONLY C, S, or straight lines -- nothing more complex
4. Body forms alternate in angle: head, ribcage, pelvis, upper leg, lower leg create ALTERNATING C-curves that lead the eye through the figure
5. Relaxed = longer flowing curves; tense = sharper bends and zig-zag
6. NEVER make forms symmetrical -- this stiffens the gesture ("snowman effect")

#### Phase 2: Spherical Forms (containment)

**Purpose**: Basic containment of forms; foundation of 3D.

**Technique**:
- Wrap gestural masses in spherical containment volumes
- The sphere establishes the VOLUME of a form before defining its exact shape
- Work AROUND and OVER the form to give volume and direction BEFORE containing the shapes

**Key rule**: The ribcage and pelvis are first indicated as TWO MASSES -- one for ribcage, one for pelvis+abdomen+buttocks. This is the primary structural decomposition.

#### Phase 3: Box Forms (spatial analysis)

**Purpose**: The box is the primary element for analysis and description of forms in real space AND the fundamental element of perspective.

**Technique**:
- Convert spherical containment volumes into box forms to establish:
  - Front/side/top plane distinctions
  - Clear spatial orientation
  - Perspective foreshortening

**Key rule**: Box forms clarify the 3D gesture and spatial placement. They answer "which way is this form facing?"

#### Phase 4: Cylinder Forms + Cross Contours

**Purpose**: Cross-contour rendering combines forms and provides traditional description of volume.

**Technique**:
- Draw lines that go ACROSS and AROUND the form (like wireframe)
- Each cross-contour shows a section of a cylinder
- Where you place the ends of cylinders and how you draw the ellipse are the main considerations for showing direction and foreshortening
- The cylinder becomes the visual structure that anatomical structure is built upon

**Material-wrapping metaphor**: Draw as if forms were covered by material. Feel where it leaves the surface of the sphere and stretches over to the edges of the box. Material coming in at the waist creates a sense of tightness.

#### Phase 5: Combining Forms (interaction)

**Purpose**: How forms react with each other to give sense of the "real."

**Key concept -- Opposites**: Forms function in showing gesture by focusing upon OPPOSITES. Where one side stretches, the other compresses. Where one form bulges, the adjacent one tucks.

#### Phase 6: Bringing Figures to Life

**Purpose**: Analysis of interaction, focusing on how forms function to show gesture through opposites.

### 2.3 Geometric Primitive Assignments

Vilppu does NOT prescribe fixed shape-to-body-part mappings. Instead, the shape depends on what the form is DOING:

| Condition | Use This Primitive |
|---|---|
| Form is contained/round | Sphere |
| Form needs spatial orientation (which way is it facing?) | Box |
| Form has length and direction | Cylinder |
| Form tapers to a point | Cone |
| Any form | Combination of the above |

**Per-body-part tendencies** (not rigid rules):

| Body Part | Typical Primitive | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ribcage | Dominant mass; sphere or box depending on pose | Largest single form in torso |
| Pelvis | Second mass; box or sphere | Connects to ribcage through flexible waist |
| Head | Sphere with box plane divisions | Similar to Loomis but less formulaic |
| Limb segments | Cylinders with elliptical cross-sections | Direction shown by ellipse orientation |
| Joints | Sphere transitions between cylinders | Where forms interact |

### 2.4 Construction Order (Complete Pipeline)

1. **Gesture**: Single flowing line capturing the action (30 seconds - 2 minutes)
2. **Two-mass indication**: Mark ribcage mass and pelvis mass as simple volumes
3. **Sphere wrapping**: Contain major forms in spherical volumes; work around/over before containing
4. **Box conversion**: Convert spheres to boxes to establish spatial orientation and plane structure
5. **Cylinder bridging**: Connect forms with cylinders; add cross-contour ellipses showing direction
6. **Anatomical landmarks**: Thoracic arch corners (ribcage bottom), iliac crest endpoints (pelvis top)
7. **Form interaction**: Refine where forms compress/stretch against each other
8. **Surface rendering**: Tone and line following the established form structure

## 3. George Bridgman

**Key texts**: *Constructive Anatomy* (1920), *Bridgman's Life Drawing* (1924), *Complete Guide to Drawing from Life* (1952)

### 3.1 Core Philosophy

**The body is a machine of interlocking wedges, boxes, and cylinders.** Bridgman sees anatomy as an engineering problem: masses wedge, mortise, and interlock. Unlike Loomis (template-first) or Vilppu (gesture-first), Bridgman is MECHANICS-first.

### 3.2 The Three-Mass System

The figure is built on THREE PRIMARY MASSES connected by a flexible spine:

| Mass | Shape | Proportions (Bridgman's scale) |
|---|---|---|
| Head | Cube/block | 1" x 5/8" (length x width) |
| Thorax (chest) | Tapered box: square at shoulders, triangular below | 1 3/4" x 1 1/4" |
| Pelvis (hips) | Square block | 1" x 1 3/8" |

**Wire model**: Bridgman prescribes building a physical model: cut three pieces of lath to these proportions, mount on flexible copper wire to represent the spine. Manipulate to observe mass relationships in space.

### 3.3 The Wedging Principle

**Central concept**: Forms do NOT simply stack -- they WEDGE into each other.

**Definition**: Masses of about the same size are conceived as ONE MASS. Masses of DIFFERENT proportions wedge into each other, mortise, or interlock.

#### Wedging chain (full body):

```
Neck (cylinder) --wedges-into--> Shoulders (square)
Shoulders (square) --wedges-into--> Ribcage (square top, triangular bottom)
Ribcage (triangular bottom) --wedges-into--> Pelvis (square)
Pelvis (square) --sits-on--> Thighs (round/cylindrical)
Thighs (round) --pass-into--> Knees (square)
Knees (square) --pass-into--> Calves (triangular)
Calves (triangular) --pass-into--> Ankles (square)
```

**Pattern**: The shape ALTERNATES: square -> triangular -> square -> round -> square -> triangular -> square. This alternation creates the visual rhythm of the figure.

### 3.4 Head Construction: The Cube Method

**Core principle**: The head is a CUBE, not a sphere. The cube inherently carries perspective, foreshortening, and mass.

#### Cube proportions:

| Dimension | Units |
|---|---|
| Front face height | 4 units |
| Front face width | 3 units |
| Depth (front to back) | ~4 units |
| Total head height | 8 units |
| Total head width | 6 units |
| Total head depth | 7.5 units |

#### Front face ratio: 1.75:1 (height:width)

#### Feature placement (4-line method):

1. **Line 1 (vertical)**: Divides face in half left/right
2. **Line 2 (horizontal)**: From ear base, perpendicular to Line 1, passes through lower eyelids. This line bisects the head top-to-bottom.
3. **Line 3**: From cheekbone width to outer chin border, establishing the angle of the facial features
4. **Line 4**: Intersects Lines 2 and 3; establishes the feature angle to nose base

**Rule**: "Whether the head is seen from above or below, the features will follow the number four line."

#### Three horizontal divisions of the front face:

1. Top half = forehead + cranium
2. Line through lower eyelids = center
3. Bottom half divides again at nose base

#### Finding the ear: Draw diagonal lines corner-to-corner on the cube's SIDE PLANE; the intersection marks ear placement.

#### Cheekbone depth: Set back approximately 1/3 of the distance from the front face to the ear.

#### Four head masses:
1. Forehead (flat/slightly convex)
2. Cheekbone region (wedge outward)
3. Cylindrical form (nose/mouth base)
4. Triangular jaw form (wedge downward)

#### Foreshortening rules for head:
- At eye level: ear base and nose base align horizontally
- Head above eye level (or tilted back): nose base rises ABOVE ear line
- Head below eye level (or tilted forward): nose base drops BELOW ear line
- The degree of separation between nose base and ear base indicates the degree of tilt

### 3.5 Torso Construction

#### Ribcage:
- **Top**: Square at the shoulders
- **Bottom**: Triangular, tapering to a wedge
- **Front**: The thoracic arch defines the bottom front edge
- **Behavior**: The ribcage acts as a BLOCK -- it can be twisted, tilted, or straight, but the block is indicated as a single unit

#### Pelvis:
- **Shape**: Square block
- **Sits on**: Round pillars of the thighs
- **Connection**: A triangular wedge from the sides of the hips enters the ribcage (the waist/oblique region)

#### Torso connection:
The flexible spine allows:
- **Flexion/extension**: Forward/backward bending (primarily lumbar when erect, middle vertebrae when half-flexed, upper vertebrae when fully bent)
- **Lateral bending**: Side-to-side
- **Rotation**: Twist between ribcage and pelvis

### 3.6 Limb Construction

#### Upper arm:
- Cylinder, viewed as a series of connected cylindrical segments
- Ball-and-socket joint at shoulder allows flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, rotation

#### Forearm:
- Cylinder that rotates (pronation/supination)
- The two bones (radius/ulna) create a wedging/crossing action

#### Leg (full chain):
- **Thigh**: Round/cylindrical form
- **Knee**: Square form (hinge joint)
- **Calf**: Triangular form (tapers)
- **Ankle**: Square form

### 3.7 Bridgman's Construction Order

1. Establish the three primary masses (head block, chest block, pelvis block)
2. Position them in space relative to each other (tilt, twist, rotation via spine)
3. Connect with construction lines showing the spine relationship
4. Add the wedging transitions between masses
5. Attach limbs as cylinders with proper joint geometry
6. Develop the interlocking/wedging of smaller forms within the larger masses
7. Add anatomical detail following the established mass structure

### 3.8 Balance Rule

**Equilibrium depends on the center of gravity passing through the supporting foot/feet.** In any standing pose, a vertical line from the pit of the neck must fall within the base of support (foot or between feet).

## 6. Foreshortening Rules (All Three)

### Universal principles:

1. **Draw THROUGH the form**: Imagine the form is transparent. Draw contours over, under, and around to understand 3D position.

2. **Overlap is the strongest depth cue**: When foreshortened, forms stack: forearm overlaps upper arm, ribcage overlaps pelvis. Getting overlaps right matters MORE than getting exact proportions right.

3. **Ellipse width indicates angle**: Cylinder viewed end-on = nearly circular ellipse. Cylinder at 45 degrees = medium ellipse. Cylinder perpendicular to view = flat line. The rounder the ellipse, the more the cylinder points at you.

4. **Closer forms are larger**: Apply consistent scale diminution. If a wrist is normally 60% of shoulder width, make it 40-45% when foreshortened away from viewer.

5. **Cross-contours wrap around form**: Every surface mark (shadow edge, wrinkle, hatching) must follow the curvature of the underlying geometric form. Shadow edges curve following form direction.

### Per-artist foreshortening method:

| Artist | Primary Tool | How It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **Loomis** | Sphere cross rotation | The cross on the head sphere rotates with 3D orientation. Thirds compress with perspective. Think in blocks and planes, not muscles. |
| **Vilppu** | Cylinder ellipses + overlap | Place cylinder ends as ellipses; rounder = more aimed at viewer. Stack overlapping forms front-to-back. Cross-contours confirm volume. |
| **Bridgman** | Cube perspective inherent | The cube automatically carries its foreshortening. The 4-line feature system follows perspective. Side planes narrow/widen with rotation. |

### Programmable foreshortening rules:

```
# Ellipse degree (0 = line, 90 = circle) based on cylinder angle to viewer
ELLIPSE_OPENNESS = sin(angle_between_cylinder_axis_and_view_direction) * 90

# Overlap priority (what's in front)
OVERLAP_RULES = {
    "arm_toward_viewer": ["hand", "forearm", "upper_arm", "shoulder"],
    "leg_toward_viewer": ["foot", "calf", "thigh", "hip"],
    "torso_toward_viewer": ["ribcage_front", "navel", "pelvis"]
}

# Scale diminution per unit depth
FORESHORTEN_SCALE = 1.0 - (depth_units * perspective_strength)
# where perspective_strength ~= 0.05 to 0.15 depending on viewing distance
```

## 7. Seeing Through Shadows to Form

### The Problem
Shadows obscure the geometric construction. Untrained eyes draw shadow shapes instead of form shapes. Edge detection (both human and machine) sees brightness boundaries, not construction boundaries.

### All Three Artists' Solution: Construction First, Shadows After

The shared principle: **Establish the geometric mass BEFORE considering light.**

| Artist | Approach |
|---|---|
| **Loomis** | Build the mannequin from eggs/cylinders/spheres first. Light is applied to already-understood forms. "You cannot draw a muscle correctly without a fair estimate of the area it occupies within the figure." |
| **Vilppu** | Gesture and construction phases are completed in LINE before any tonal rendering. Modeling tone (Phase 7) comes only after all geometric structure is established. Shadows follow the cross-contour directions already laid down. |
| **Bridgman** | The cube/block construction IS the form -- shadows are merely the unlit planes of already-established blocks. "The block carries with it from any angle its perspective and its foreshortening, and it carries with itself the sense of mass." Shadow edges are where planes change direction. |

### Programmable Rules for Form vs. Shadow:

```
# Shadow edges that indicate FORM (keep these):
FORM_EDGES = [
    "plane_change",           # where a surface changes direction
    "silhouette_contour",     # outer edge of the form against background
    "cross_contour_boundary"  # where one form overlaps another
]

# Shadow edges that indicate LIGHT (ignore for construction):
LIGHT_EDGES = [
    "cast_shadow",            # shadow thrown BY one form ONTO another
    "ambient_occlusion",      # darkening in crevices
    "specular_highlight_edge" # bright spot boundary
]

# Rule: When analyzing a reference image for construction:
# 1. Identify FORM EDGES first (these define the geometric masses)
# 2. Classify shadows: is this edge caused by a plane change or by lighting?
# 3. Plane-change shadows = structural (use for construction)
# 4. Cast shadows = lighting artifact (ignore for construction)
# 5. Build the bill of materials (geometric primitives) BEFORE applying any shading
```

### Trust Assessment:

- **Art Students League (LINEA)**: HIGH trust. Historical institutional source.

### Limitations:
- Vilppu's method resists full formalization because he explicitly rejects rigid rules. The pipeline order is extractable but the specific form assignments are intentionally contextual.
- Bridgman's exact proportional ratios for the full body (beyond head and three-mass scale model) are sparse in secondary sources. His books rely heavily on ILLUSTRATION rather than numerical specification.
- Loomis's mannequin proportions are the most numerically complete and directly programmable.
- None of these artists provide exact ANGLE values for joint rotations or tilt relationships. The angular relationships are described qualitatively ("the ribcage tilts opposite to the pelvis") rather than as degree ranges.
